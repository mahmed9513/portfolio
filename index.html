Link2 San Diego

User Research, Website Design

Summary

I was part of a team of four undergraduate students working on behalf of the UCSD Design Lab to redesign the website for Link2 San Diego, a program launched by the San Diego Regional EDC. The goal was to create a website that would introduce students in San Diego to new industries and career opportunities in the region and promote networking events.

My Role

I acted primarily as a user researcher. My work included: basic needfinding, developing card sorting tasks, conducting user interviews and competitive analysis, and creating user profiles. I also created style tiles and wireframes as well as design elements for the high-fidelity prototypes.

Competitive Analysis

We began our research by conducting a competitive analysis of industry labels to see how our client’s labels compared with those of business professionals in other regions. We used this analysis to choose the labels we believed were the easiest to understand and the most representative of the college majors associated with each industry.

Card Sorting

We used the results of the competitive analysis to create a closed card sorting task. This was done on a Trello board with the industry labels chosen from the competitive analysis and a list of college majors that were featured on our client’s site. We asked participants to sort the majors into the industries as best as they could. The results helped us define the content associated with each label. We had some surprising results. For example, many participants chose to list Maritime Engineering under Aerospace and Defense instead of Manufacturing.

User Interviews and Personas

We then conducted a number of user interviews with undergraduate students to better contextualize our design. We asked questions regarding how they searched for jobs and pursued employment opportunities in order to better understand the potential use cases for our website. The interview results were consolidated into multiple personas, such as the one shown above, to present to our client.

Style Tiles

We created style tiles for our client in order to receive feedback on the visual design of the site before adding content to it. The biggest challenge was finding colors that would pop without clashing with the colors of our client’s logo. We also wanted to present the site as youthful to attract students but keep an air of professionalism for potential industry partners. After presenting multiple palettes and styles to our client, we decided to minimize the use of color to one dark color, one light color, and a single accent color to highlight key words and interactions.

Wireframes

Once we finalized the color and styling with our client, we began developing wireframes to get feedback on the content layout of the site in greater detail. We incorporated feedback from the user interviews and our client to determine the features we included. The biggest challenge was deciding how to design the homepage to best highlight all the industries, our client’s industry partners, and the events they hosted without overwhelming our users. After many rounds of deliberation and client feedback, we decided to place the industry descriptions on the homepage and have different pages for our client’s industry partners and hosted events.

Final Design

The final product is a responsive website with a simple color scheme and many features, including industry descriptions, user testimonials, an events calendar, and a custom feedback survey form.

RoadRunner

Mobile Web Development

Summary

This application was developed for a 10-week Human-Computer Interaction Programming Studio course. The challenge was to design and develop and modern mobile web application with a focus on geographical and map-related applications. We created RoadRunner, an app for creating, customizing, and sharing day trip itineraries using a custom mapping interface.

My Role

I acted primarily as a front-end web developer. My focus was on creating the interface components for the apps using HTML, CSS/Sass, and Javascript. I also implemented Google Maps API to support the core map functionality of the app.

Problem

Currently, planning fun day trips requires the use of multiple applications such as Google Maps, TripAdvisor, and Yelp. This can make the planning process lengthy and difficult, especially if the user is trying to explore new and unfamiliar places. TripAdvisor is hailed as one of the best trip planning services available, yet it has no feature for connecting multiple points of interest on a map and providing directions to each point. We believe this is an important feature to have as it encourages users to explore more than one place and to think about a trip as more than just traveling from point A to point B.

Solution

We created an app that incorporates features from Google Maps, TripAdvisor, and Yelp to create an all-in-one experience that makes trip planning easy and fun! With our app, users can create their own lists of destinations, as well as search for other places they're interested in and discover lists for those places, all on one website. We also allowed users to share and explore other users' lists to encourage social interaction.

OSP Public Health Tool

Data Analysis, Web Development

Summary

This application was developed for a 10-week Design for Development course. The challenge was to create a tool that would aid our client, One Step Projects, in their humanitarian efforts in Nepal. We created the OSP Public Health Tool, a web application for storing, organizing, and visualizing Nepali public health data.

My Role

I was the design lead for the project team. My primary responsibility was to guide the team through the human-centered design process and outline weekly progress to ensure proper allocation of time and resources. I also worked on front-end development for the application.

Problem

Our client, One Step Projects (OSP), is an organization that hopes to improve social and environmental conditions throughout the world with the planning of various developmental projects. OSP members were working to rebuild communities in Nepal destroyed by the string of earthquakes in 2015. A major obstacle they encounter when fighting for change in a country like Nepal is a limited understanding of the spread of various diseases and the availability and condition of health and medicinal resources.

Solution

To provide OSP members with a convenient way to gather and visualize information about the various health issues in Nepal, we proposed an implementation of a website with three core features: heat maps that allow OSP members to view concentrations of data in different regions of Nepal, data tables and a search bar to organize and parse through the different data sets, and an import button to input newly collected data from external sources. Our final design was the result of weeks of planning, a dozen stakeholder interviews, and a multitude of user tests on our prototypes.
